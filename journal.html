Featured read

The parts you do not see are usually the parts that decide whether a game survives.

The visible parts of a prototype get all the attention first, but the hidden structure is usually what decides whether the project survives. Combat feel, effects, menus, and movement can all look promising early while blurry ownership rules and messy state flow quietly make every future change more expensive.

The warning signs are always similar: one fix creates side effects, new features take too long, and nobody can point clearly to the source of truth. In games this pain arrives fast, which is why I treat architecture less as elegance and more as momentum protection.

If I can tell where authority lives, inspect the system cleanly, and change it without negotiating with old shortcuts, the project stays alive. That is the standard I try to build toward now.

Featured read

Stray animals, loud opinions, and the kind of decency I actually trust.

The internet makes opinions cheap. It is easy to sound morally serious in public, and much harder to stay decent in ordinary moments when nobody is watching closely.

One small test I keep returning to is how people treat stray animals. Not as performance, but as instinct. Whether they notice, pause, and make room for something vulnerable tells me more than polished statements usually do.

The kind of decency I trust is quiet, repetitive, and inconvenient. It shows up in habits, not slogans, and it survives privacy just as well as it survives applause.
